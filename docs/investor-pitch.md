# SLIDE 1 — The Opportunity

## What We Do

We acquire single-family residential properties in the Dallas–Fort Worth market using
all-cash purchases — no mortgage, no bank involvement, no foreclosure risk. Properties
are held approximately 5 years and then sold at market price.

Properties are selected from a live database of 6,400+ active DFW listings, screened
for rent yield, condition, and long-term appreciation potential. The Deal Manager
(Bryan Chase) makes the final acquisition decision.

All day-to-day property operations — tenant screening, leasing, rent collection,
maintenance, and evictions — are handled entirely by a licensed professional property
management company: **PPMG or PAMS Texas**, depending on location. Their fee is
8–9% of gross monthly rent and is an operating expense of the LLC.

## Why This Structure

- **All-cash** means faster closes, stronger offers, and no financing risk
- **No mortgage** means all rental income flows to investors, not a bank
- **Fixed-return investors** (debt role) earn 7.23%/yr regardless of appreciation
- **Equity investors** participate fully in appreciation and rental upside
- **Bryan earns a bonus only if the deal is profitable** — if investors don't do
  well, neither does he
- **One LLC per property** — clean liability isolation, pass-through taxation,
  no corporate tax layer

## Legal & Entity Structure

Because the properties are in Texas and the investors and Deal Manager are based
in California, two layers of legal compliance are required: Texas property law and
California securities law. The recommended structure uses two entities per deal:

**Entity 1 — Texas LLC (property-holding entity)**
Holds title to the property. One formed per property. Governed by Texas law.
Must be registered as a foreign LLC in California because it is managed from
California — this triggers California's $800/year franchise tax.

**Entity 2 — California LLC (optional, recommended for Bryan)**
A separate California management entity owned solely by Bryan through which
he acts as Deal Manager across multiple properties. Provides liability separation
between his personal assets and his management activities, and clarifies his
role in future deals without re-documenting each time.

## Attorneys Required

This deal requires two attorneys working together — one in each state:

**Texas real estate attorney**
Drafts the Texas LLC operating agreement, handles the property closing, and
ensures the entity structure is sound under Texas law.

**California securities attorney**
Ensures the investment offer complies with California's Corporate Securities
Law of 1968 (one of the strictest securities frameworks in the U.S.), handles
federal Regulation D compliance, and prepares required state and federal filings.
This attorney should be engaged **before** any offer is made to any investor.

## Agreed Operating Terms

**Capital calls (unbudgeted repairs)**
Any unbudgeted capital expenditure exceeding $2,500 requires a majority vote of
equity investors. All costs are shared equally — 50% each — by both equity investors.
The Deal Manager may authorize repairs up to $2,500 without a vote.

**Quarterly reporting**
Within 30 days of each quarter end, all investors receive: profit & loss statement,
cash flow summary, distribution detail, and any material property updates.

**Early exit**
An investor wishing to exit before year 5 has the right to sell their position and
the obligation to find a qualified, approved replacement. The exiting investor bears
all transfer legal costs (~$1,500–2,500). If no replacement is found within 90 days,
the group votes on an early sale.

**Distressed property exit**
If cumulative unbudgeted capital calls exceed **10% of the original purchase price**
in any rolling 24-month period, either equity investor may call a Distress Vote.
A vote of one equity investor is sufficient to force a market-price sale within
180 days. No investor can block this sale once the threshold is triggered. Debt
investors cannot block a distress sale but are repaid in full from proceeds before
equity investors receive anything.

*Example: On a $289,000 property, $28,900 in unexpected capital calls over 24 months
triggers the right to force a sale.*

**Dispute resolution**
Mediation first; binding arbitration if unresolved. Court litigation is a last resort.

# SLIDE 3 — Bryan's Promote: Option C (30%)

*This is the only mechanism by which Bryan earns more than the silent equity investor.*

## During the Hold: Bryan and the silent equity investor are identical

Both receive 50% of monthly net cash flow. Bryan earns no premium for his
management work during the hold period. His additional compensation is deferred
entirely to the exit — and only materializes if the deal performs.

## At Sale: The 30% Promote

**The hurdle:** Total equity invested × 7.23% × 5 years

This is the same annual rate paid to debt investors. Both equity investors must
first earn the equivalent of the debt rate on their equity before Bryan's bonus
activates. This is a high bar — intentionally.

**If total equity profit is below the hurdle:** 50/50 split, no bonus. Bryan and
the silent equity investor receive identical returns.

**If total equity profit exceeds the hurdle:**
- Bryan receives 30% of the excess above the hurdle (his promote)
- The remaining 70% of excess is split 50/50 as normal
- Result: Bryan earns more than the silent investor, but only on genuinely
  profitable deals

**If the deal loses money:** both equity investors lose equally, 50/50. No promote.

## Worked Example

> $300K property · $103K total equity · Deal generates $50K total equity profit

| | Amount |
|---|---|
| Hurdle (7.23% × $103K × 5yr) | $37,300 |
| Profit above hurdle (excess) | $12,700 |
| Silent investor receives | $37,300÷2 + $12,700×70%÷2 = **$23,095** |
| Bryan receives | $37,300÷2 + $12,700×70%÷2 + $12,700×30% = **$26,905** |
| **Bryan's bonus above a straight 50/50 split** | **$3,810** |

# APPENDIX — Legal Compliance Notes (California + Federal)

*This section is for reference and attorney review. It does not constitute legal advice.*

## Why California Law Applies

Even though the properties are in Texas, California securities law governs this
offering because the Deal Manager and most investors are California residents.
California's Corporate Securities Law of 1968 is one of the most investor-protective
securities frameworks in the United States — more stringent in several respects
than federal law. Compliance with both is required.

---

## Federal Securities Law — Regulation D, Rule 506(b)

Selling investment interests (LLC membership units) to investors is a securities
offering under federal law, regardless of how it is structured.

**Recommended exemption: Reg D Rule 506(b)**

- Allows up to 35 non-accredited investors plus unlimited accredited investors
- No general solicitation or advertising permitted — investors must have a
  pre-existing, substantive relationship with Bryan before being approached
- Requires a **Form D filing with the SEC** within 15 days of the first investment
- No SEC registration required; no approval needed — it is a notice filing only
- Cost: free to file; attorney time to prepare ~$500–1,000

**Accredited investor definition (either of the following):**
- Net worth exceeding $1,000,000 excluding primary residence, OR
- Annual income exceeding $200,000 (individual) or $300,000 (joint with spouse)
  in each of the last two years, with reasonable expectation of the same this year

If all investors are accredited, compliance is simpler. If any investor is
non-accredited, additional disclosure obligations apply (similar to a prospectus).
Strongly recommended to limit this offering to accredited investors only.

---

## California Securities Law — Corporate Securities Law of 1968

**Recommended exemption: Section 25102(f) — Limited Offering Exemption**

Available when the offering is made to 35 or fewer California residents who either:
- Have a pre-existing personal or business relationship with Bryan, OR
- By reason of their business or financial experience, can reasonably be assumed
  capable of evaluating the merits and risks of the investment

Requirements:
- **No advertising or general solicitation** of any kind
- **Notice filing with the California DFPI** (Department of Financial Protection
  and Innovation) within 15 days of the first sale in California
- Filing fee: approximately $300–600
- The offering must be made in compliance with Reg D 506(b) concurrently

**Alternative: Section 25102(n) — Accredited Investor Exemption**

If every California investor is accredited (as defined above), this exemption
is simpler and has fewer procedural requirements. Also requires a DFPI notice
filing and fee. Recommended if investor pool will be exclusively accredited.

---

## California Franchise Tax — Foreign LLC Registration

A Texas LLC managed from California (Bryan is the manager and is California-based)
is considered to be "doing business in California" under California law. This
requires the Texas LLC to register as a **foreign LLC** with the California
Secretary of State and pay California's **$800/year minimum franchise tax**,
regardless of whether the LLC earns any California income.

This applies to each property LLC individually. For two properties: $1,600/yr.

The LLC will also be required to file a California Form 568 (LLC Return of Income)
each year. California investors will receive a K-1 and will owe California income
tax on their share of rental income and capital gains, even though the income is
generated in Texas. Texas has no state income tax, so there is no offset.

---

## California Community Property Notice

California is a community property state. If any investor is married and a
California resident, their investment may be considered community property. The
Operating Agreement and subscription documents should include a spousal consent
and acknowledgment clause for married California investors. The California
securities attorney will handle this.
